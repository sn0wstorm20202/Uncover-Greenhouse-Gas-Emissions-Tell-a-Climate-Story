Greenhouse Gases

and their Impact on Climate Change.

INTRODUCTION:

Climate change is one of the most pressing global challenges of our time. The primary driver behind this phenomenon is the increase in greenhouse gases (GHGs) in the atmosphere, which trap heat from the Sun and contribute to the Earth's rising temperatures.

Greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide (CO₂) and methane (CH₄) play a critical role in climate change by enhancing the atmosphere's ability to trap heat, leading to a cascade of environmental impacts.

  • Greenhouse gases (GHGs) are gases that trap heat from the Sun, contributing to the greenhouse effect that warms the Earth’s atmosphere.
  • Fossil fuels, such as petroleum, coal, and natural gas, are hydrocarbons that, when burned for energy, release significant amounts of CO₂ into the atmosphere.
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is a naturally occurring gas, but human activities— such as the burning of fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial processes, are leading to a sharp rise in CO₂ levels. CO₂ is the primary contributor to global warming.
  • Methane (CH₄), another potent greenhouse gas, is the major component of natural gas and is released through agricultural practices, the energy sector, and naturally occurring processes like the decay of organic matter in wetlands.

The burning of fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial activities act as GHG sources, adding harmful gases to the atmosphere. Conversely, ecosystems such as forests act as GHG sinks, absorbing carbon dioxide through photosynthesis and storing it in plants and soils. Understanding the delicate balance between these sources and sinks is key to addressing climate change.

To manage and limit global warming, the concept of a carbon budget is crucial. The global carbon budget assesses sources and sinks of carbon and tracks changes in atmospheric CO₂ levels. The total carbon budget refers to the maximum allowable human-caused CO₂ emissions if we are to keep global warming within safe limits, such as the 1.5°C or 2°C targets set by international agreements.

Different methods are used to estimate emissions, including top-down estimates, which derive data from atmospheric observations, and bottom-up estimates, based on land, ocean models, or human activity data. Lateral fluxes represent the movement of carbon between different ecosystems, such as the flow of carbon from land to ocean systems via rivers.

Our project focuses on visualizing these complex relationships by providing an accessible platform where users can explore year-on-year greenhouse gas emissions by country and by industry. This interactive map allows anyone, from policymakers to the general public, to better understand the trends in emissions and their impact on climate change. By offering a detailed analysis of emissions data, we hope to contribute to the global effort to combat climate change through informed decision-making and public awareness.

IMPACTS:

Over the past decade, we have witnessed unprecedented changes in our climate, including rising temperatures, extreme weather events, and disruptions to natural ecosystems. As GHG emissions continue to rise, so do the associated risks— ranging from rising sea levels to food insecurity and the displacement of vulnerable populations. The ability to track and analyze emissions at both the country and industry levels is critical to understanding how these emissions contribute to climate change.

Our project goes beyond simply presenting data; it provides actionable insights. By visualizing year-on-year changes in emissions and breaking them down by industry, we offer a tool for policymakers, researchers, and advocates to pinpoint the areas of highest concern. Understanding these trends is the first step toward designing effective climate policies, implementing sustainable practices, and transitioning to a low-carbon economy.

The analysis of GHG emissions reveals key trends that help us understand the sources and drivers of climate change.

Here are some of the findings we highlight in our project:

  1. Country-Level Emissions: Our color gradient map shows a stark contrast between emission levels in developed and developing nations. The data highlights that the largest emitters, such as the U.S., China, and India, contribute significantly to global emissions due to industrial activity, energy consumption, and transportation.
  2. Industry-Specific Emissions: Upon clicking on a specific country, our platform reveals the breakdown of emissions by industry. This includes data on sectors such as energy, transportation, agriculture, and manufacturing, which are the major contributors to GHG emissions. For example, in many countries, the energy sector is responsible for the largest share of emissions due to reliance on fossil fuels for power generation.
  3. Year-on-Year Trends: By analyzing year-on-year changes in emissions, we can identify patterns of increase or decrease in specific regions and industries. This analysis is crucial for evaluating the effectiveness of climate policies, monitoring progress toward emission reduction targets, and identifying areas that require urgent attention.
